Analyzed numerical simulation behavior and floating-point precision pitfallsAnalyzed numerical simulation behavior and floating-point precision pitfallsThis solves the 1D linear reaction–diffusion equation
∂u/∂t = D ∂²u/∂x² + λu on x∈[0,L], with Dirichlet BCs u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0
using explicit FTCS (forward Euler in time, central difference in space). The initial data is the fundamental mode u(x,0)=sin(πx/L), which already satisfies the BCs.
Discretization. With grid spacing dx = L/(N-1) and step dt = T/M, the interior update is the standard stencil
u_i^{n+1} = u_i^n + r·(u_{i+1}^n − 2u_i^n + u_{i-1}^n) + λΔt·u_i^n
where r = D·dt/dx² (D_dt_over_dx2) and λΔt (lambda_dt) are precomputed. Boundaries are pinned to 0 each step; u_current = u_next swaps by copy. print_max_value just reports max over the grid every M/50 steps.
The numbers. L=1, N=101 ⇒ dx=0.01; M=80000, T=4 ⇒ dt=5e-5. So r = 0.01·5e-5/1e-4 = 0.005 (diffusion number, CFL needs ≤0.5 — very safe). λΔt = 1.25e-3.
What actually happens. This is a separable linear problem: the sine mode is preserved and its amplitude evolves as exp((λ − D(π/L)²)t). Here D(π/L)² ≈ 0.0987, so the net rate is ≈ 24.90, and at T=4 you expect exp(99.6) ≈ 1.8e43. I ran it to confirm — double gives max = 1.70e43 at t=4 (discrete Laplacian eigenvalue is marginally below −π², hence slightly under the continuum prediction). The peak sits at x=0.5.
On the stability warning — it's misleading. The code's stability_limit = 1/(2D/dx² + λ) ≈ 4.44e-3 is satisfied (dt=5e-5 ≪), and the amplification factor of even the checkerboard mode is 1 + λΔt − 4rΔt/dx²·… = 0.981 (|g|<1). There is no numerical instability here. The "blow-up to inf" is the true solution growing exponentially, not an FTCS artifact. The warning text conflates the two.
The FP-relevant part (the typedef you'd flip). Under float, max(u) reaches FLT_MAX ≈ 3.4e38 around ln(3.4e38)/24.90 ≈ 3.56, then overflows to +inf. My run confirms first non-finite max at t=3.566 (step 71304). One step later the diffusion stencil computes inf − 2·inf + inf → NaN, which propagates across the interior. And note the silent failure mode: std::max_element uses operator<, every comparison against NaN is false, so it never advances past begin() — which is the pinned boundary 0.0. The float run therefore reports a final max of 0.0, not inf and not NaN. A monitor keying on inf/NaN in the reported value would miss it entirely; the poison is upstream. (Relevant to the NaN/Inf-suppression angle you work on.)
Two smaller notes: the second-difference u_{i+1}−2u_i+u_{i-1} is a cancellation of nearly-equal large operands — for this smooth mode it's ~1e-3 relative, so you shed 3–4 digits in the diffusion term. It doesn't matter for the max because diffusion is ~250× smaller than reaction here, but in float it means the diffusion contribution is essentially noise. Design rationale (the short version). nixnan buckets both exceptions and the exponent histogram by the SASS instruction's format (f16/bf16/f32/f64). So to get clean per-format attribution you want the arithmetic to stay natively in each type — no float accumulation sneaking in — and you want the three formats in separate kernels so their (very different) overflow timelines don't interleave in one instruction stream. Same FTCS reaction–diffusion step, three precisions, three kernels. That directly answers your question: yes, three separate kernels is the right call, both for attribution and because each format hits its edge at a completely different step count, so independent time-stepping loops let each run to its own blow-up.
The precision-edge math is what makes it a good histogram demo. With the tutorial preset below (D=0.01, λ=50, dx=0.01, dt=1e-3, so per-step growth ≈ 1.05×), the center node climbs one FP binade roughly every ~14 steps and marches straight up the exponent axis:

FP16 (5-bit exp, max 65504=2¹⁶): overflows to inf at step ≈227 (t≈0.23). Also — the diffusion increment r·(u_{i+1}−2u_i+u_{i-1}) is ≈2⁻¹³·u, which for the near-boundary nodes lands below 2⁻¹⁴ and shows up as subnormals: the diffusion physics is silently flushed while reaction runs away. Two exception classes from one kernel.
BF16 (8-bit exp, same range as FP32): no overflow until step ≈1818 (t≈1.82), but the second-difference cancellation with 8 mantissa bits means the diffusion term is essentially noise the whole way — a precision failure the exception counters won't flag, only the histogram's width will hint at it.
FP32: same overflow step as BF16 (≈1818), clean mantissa. The contrast BF16-vs-FP32 at identical exponent range is the teaching moment.
